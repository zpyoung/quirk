# Assumption Reversal

## When to Use
Reach for this when a problem space feels saturated and every idea looks like a variation on what already exists. It works best once you can articulate how the category "normally" operates, because the technique mines those defaults for hidden constraints. Use it to break out of incremental thinking and find structurally different designs rather than feature tweaks.

## The Method
1. State the problem, then list every assumption the field or product treats as obvious — about the user, the workflow, the data, the business model, and the constraints.
2. Negate each assumption literally, even the ones that feel absurd ("users must log in" becomes "users never log in").
3. For each reversal, ask what design becomes necessary or possible if it were true, and sketch that design concretely.
4. Keep the reversals that produce a coherent, buildable concept and discard the ones that collapse on contact.
5. Pull the surviving ideas back toward reality, importing only the constraints you actually need rather than all of them.

## Example
A team building a note-taking app lists its assumptions: notes are saved by the user, organized in folders, and owned by one account. They reverse "the user organizes their notes" to "the app never asks the user to organize anything." That forces a design with no folders, no tags, no manual filing — everything is captured into one stream and surfaced later by search and automatic linking. The reversal produced the core insight behind backlink-based tools: structure emerges from connections between notes instead of from a hierarchy the user maintains.

## Why It Works
Expertise compresses repeated decisions into invisible defaults, so the most limiting constraints are the ones you stop seeing. Forcing a literal negation makes those defaults visible and legal to question, which is the core mechanic of de Bono's lateral thinking and reverse brainstorming. The reversed assumption acts as a generative seed, pointing toward designs that the unexamined default had quietly ruled out.
