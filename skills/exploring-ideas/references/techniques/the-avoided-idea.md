# The Avoided Idea

## When to Use
Reach for this when the conversation keeps orbiting one option without ever landing on it — when proposals stay safe, hedged, or incremental and the room feels like it is holding something back. It is especially useful late in a session, after the obvious ideas are exhausted and the group has settled into agreeable consensus. The signal to use it is a faint shared sense that the most interesting move is also the one no one wants to be the first to say.

## The Method
1. Name the avoidance out loud: state that there is an idea everyone is circling but no one has proposed, and make surfacing it the explicit goal.
2. Apply a forcing constraint — "if this were the last idea we were allowed to propose, what would it be?" — to strip away the option of staying neutral.
3. Have each person write down the one idea that feels slightly embarrassing or too bold to say, before anyone speaks, so social hedging does not pre-filter it.
4. Voice the cringe candidates without softening or qualifying them; treat discomfort as evidence the idea touches something real, not as a reason to discard it.
5. Pick the strongest avoided idea and commit to it fully for the rest of the session — design it as if it will ship, rather than debating whether it should.

## Example
A team building a developer CLI keeps proposing config flags, better error messages, and a docs overhaul to fix low adoption. Everyone is circling a different idea but no one says it. Under the "last idea" constraint, someone finally voices it: delete the config file entirely and make the tool zero-config with sane inferred defaults. It feels reckless — years of flags represent real user investment. But committing to it surfaces that 90% of configs were identical, and the embarrassing idea becomes a `--strict` escape hatch plus inference, cutting onboarding from 20 minutes to one command.

## Why It Works
What suppresses bold ideas in groups is rarely a judgment that they lack merit — it is anticipated social cost, the fear of looking naive or of owning a risky call. By naming the avoidance and removing the neutral option, the technique separates fear of judgment from actual evaluation, so the idea gets assessed on substance. This is psychological-safety ideation paired with a forced constraint: scarcity ("the last idea") raises the value of candor above the value of self-protection.
