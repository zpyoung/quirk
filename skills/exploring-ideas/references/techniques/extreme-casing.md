# Extreme-Casing

## When to Use
Reach for this when an idea has stalled at a safe, incremental version and no one can articulate what a bolder move would even look like. It is most useful early in exploration, when the goal is to widen the option space rather than converge. Use it when the team keeps proposing variations that all sit within a few percent of the status quo.

## The Method
1. State the idea in its current, cautious form as a single sentence.
2. Identify the one dimension that defines it — speed, scope, price, frequency, automation, constraint — and name the direction it could be pushed.
3. Push that dimension to its most extreme version that still technically functions. Not "faster," but "instant"; not "fewer steps," but "zero steps."
4. Describe what the product, workflow, or system would actually have to be to deliver that extreme — every assumption it breaks.
5. Walk back toward the middle and stop at the first version that keeps the extreme's core insight but is buildable.
6. Compare that recovered middle path against the original cautious idea; ship the one that is genuinely better, not the one that is more familiar.

## Example
Ordinary starting point: a SaaS onboarding flow with a 12-step setup wizard the team wants to "shorten to maybe 8 steps."
The move: push to the extreme — zero setup steps. The product must be fully usable the instant the user signs up, with no configuration at all.
Forcing that extreme exposes the real assumption: configuration exists because the app cannot infer the user's intent. To hit zero steps, you would have to derive sane defaults from the signup context (company domain, plan, referral source) and let users correct later.
Recovered middle path: a working default workspace generated automatically, with configuration demoted to optional in-context nudges. The result is a 2-step flow that converts better than either the 12-step original or a naive 8-step trim — an insight the "shorten it" framing would never have produced.

## Why It Works
Incremental framing anchors thinking to the current solution, so every option clusters near it. Pushing a single dimension to its functional limit detaches you from that anchor and forces you to confront the assumptions the original quietly depends on. This is a scaling provocation in the lateral-thinking tradition: the absurd extreme is never the answer, but it reveals the structure of the problem, and the recovered middle inherits that clarity.
