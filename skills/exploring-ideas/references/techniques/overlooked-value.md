# Overlooked Value

## When to Use
Reach for this when the obvious, exciting parts of the problem are already crowded with ideas and competitors, and the session keeps circling the same glamorous surface. It is the right move when you suspect the real leverage is hiding in the part everyone treats as plumbing, overhead, or a necessary evil. Use it to turn a dismissed corner of the problem space into the differentiating asset.

## The Method
1. List every part of the problem space, then mark the parts that feel boring, ugly, manual, or beneath attention — the ones the team skips past in five seconds.
2. Pick the single most ignored item and state plainly why everyone overlooks it.
3. Invert the dismissal: assume this exact part is where the hidden value lives, and force at least three reasons that could be true.
4. Find who suffers most from the current neglect, and design the version of the product where this part is the best part, not the afterthought.
5. Pressure-test it: would making this the centerpiece create a moat competitors won't bother to copy?

## Example
A team building a CI/CD product fixates on faster build times — the crowded, glamorous metric everyone benchmarks. The overlooked part: the build *failure* experience, just a red X and a 4,000-line log nobody reads. They invert it and make failures the centerpiece — automatic log parsing that surfaces the three relevant lines, a one-click "re-run from failed step," and a diff showing what changed since the last green build. Builds are no faster, but the painful 20 minutes of log-spelunking after every failure nearly vanishes. Competitors keep optimizing speed; this team owns the moment developers actually feel pain.

## Why It Works
Attention is allocated by convention, so the parts everyone agrees to ignore accumulate unsolved pain and zero competition — value concentrates precisely where nobody looks. Deliberately reframing a dismissed constraint as the central asset breaks the anchoring that made it invisible. This is constraint-as-asset reframing, a lateral-thinking move that escapes the dominant pattern by attacking what the pattern discards.
